Sicilian Defense

The Sicilian Defense is a King’s Pawn Opening beginning with 1. e4 c5

By: Fabian Steineck

February 28, 2023

chess strategy games chess tactics

Black, like in symmetrical King's Pawn openings like the Ruy Lopez, secures some central space by occupying the center on move one. However, in the Sicilian Defense, black ensures that the position becomes unbalanced!

For several decades, the Sicilian Defense weapon has been a popular fighting weapon at all levels of chess.

In comparison to 1...e5, the Sicilian Defense has the disadvantage of not opening up any lines for black's pieces to develop (except for the queen, which often does not want to develop too early). This means that in the Sicilian Defense, white will frequently gain an advantage in piece development... But how can White take advantage of this?

White can try to open the center with an early d4, hoping to use their more active pieces... However, there is a positional disadvantage. If white plays d4, black can exchange their c-pawn for white's more central d-pawn, giving them the long-term positional advantage of having two central pawns to one!

We'll look at a few different strategies white can use against the Sicilian Defense:

• White quickly opens the center with Nf3 and d4 (The Open Sicilian)

• White quickly opens the center with c3 and d4 (Alapin Variation)

• White plays d4 immediately (Smith-Morra Gambit)

• White does not open the center and instead builds up slowly on the kingside where there is more room (Closed Sicilian)

1. e4 c5 2. Nf3 d6 3. d4 cxd4 4. Nxd4 – The Open Sicilian

Note that black has a couple options on move two. 2...d6 is popular, but so are 2...e6 and 2...Nc6. There are numerous transpositions between the three options, but many continuations are unique to each.

The Open Sicilian is a highly complex opening with at least a dozen subvariations that is not suitable for beginners on either side! White accepts black's challenge and immediately opens the center, attempting to capitalize on their development lead.

The following are some common themes in the Open Sicilian:

• If black castles kingside, White may castle queenside in some lines, leaving the kingside pawns free to advance towards the enemy king on g8.

• From afar, black frequently attempts to exert pressure on white's center. Playing...a6 and...b5 followed by a later...b4 is a common strategy for removing white's knight from c3 and weakening white's defense of e4.

• Black may play a later...e5 to equalize the space in the center, but must ensure that their d-pawn does not become weak!

Let's look at one exciting, aggressive continuation to show how dangerous the Open Sicilian can be!

1. E4 c5 2. Nf3 d6 3. d4 cxd4 4. Nxd4 Nf6 5. Nc3 a6

The Najdorf Variation of the Sicilian Defense is very popular. Playing an early flank pawn move like...a6 may appear to violate the Opening Principles, but black wants to deny the white pieces the b5 square and eventually expand with...b5.

5…g6 would instead be the infamous Dragon variation

6. Be3 e5 7. Nb3 Be6 8. f3 Be7 9. Qd2 Nbd7 10. 0-0-0 0-0

The stage is set. White’s set-up, categorized by the early f3 and queenside castling, is known as the English Attack against the Najdorf Sicilian (yes, even the subvariations have subvariations!) White wants to advance his kingside pawns towards the enemy monarch, and to do so without exposing his own king, white castles in the other direction. The f3 pawn does a good job of stabilizing the center as well as supporting the advance of the g-pawn.

11. g4 b5 12. g5 b4 13. Ne2 Ne8 14. f4 a5 15. f5 a4

Insanity! Both sides are charging headlong towards the opposing king. White's strongest move here is 16. Nbd4!, which temporarily sacrifices a piece, but after 16...exd4 17. Nxd4, black's bishop is still under attack, and white threatens the powerful fork Nc6. White will reclaim the piece...in a highly asymmetrical, double-edged position.

This is just one exciting and dangerous variation of the Open Sicilian - beware!

Other versions of the Open Sicilian, however, (such as the Maroczy Bind in the Accelerated Dragon) are much more positional in nature - but no less strategically demanding. Many variations (such as the Sveshnikov) have uncommon middlegames not found in other opening lines!

The Taimanov Sicilian is a variation on the Open Sicilian in which the queen comes to c7 early on!

1. e4 c5 2. c3 – The Alapin Variation

In comparison to the thrills of the Open Sicilian, the Alapin variation is much more approachable to those who don't want to go down the rabbit hole of opening memorization.

White's strategy is sound and straightforward: white wants to play d4 next turn and capture the center, and if black exchanges on d4, white will be able to recapture with a pawn rather than a piece.

This strategy works well against an unprepared Black player. My first tournament chess game ever started with 1. e4 c5 2. c3 Nc6 3. d4 cxd4 4. cxd4 Nf6 5. Nc3:

Success! Complete central control.

But black can fight back better than this. The most significant disadvantage of 2. c3 is that it removes the best square for white's queenside knight, which black can exploit. 2...d5! is a common response (others include 2...Nf6 and 2...e6).

Black's queen is much more secure in the center after 3. exd5 Qxd5, as white cannot play Nc3 and kick it away!

The Alapin variation probably doesn't give white more than equality if black knows how to handle it, but it's easy to learn and difficult to screw up.

1. e4 c5 2. d4 – The Smith Morra Gambit

In the Smith-Morra, white tries to extend his development lead even further by playing d4 without any preparation. White intends to open up the position by sacrificing a pawn.

2…cxd4 3. c3

Of course, white could take back with the queen on move 3, but this is unlikely. Black will simply get to play... Nc6 and kick the queen away while gaining time, and white is simply playing a weaker version of the above-mentioned Open Sicilian.

This pawn sacrifice is white’s idea behind the second move.

3…dxc3 4. Nxc3

White is down a pawn, but has opportunities to gain a significant development advantage. White's rooks will eventually exert pressure along the semi-open d-and-c files, and black must be cautious about how they develop! For instance, after 4... 5. Nc6 d6 6. Bc4 Nf6?!

7. e5! Is very strong. 7…dxe5 8. Qxd8+ Nxd8 9. Nb5! The position has opened up long before black’s pieces were ready to fight.

With accurate play, Black can defend against the Smith-Morra, but it will be a difficult task. Many strong players refuse to accept the "free" pawn, resulting in a position similar to the Alapin variation described above.

Closed Sicilian

White can avoid all three of the above ideas by not playing an early d4, keeping the center closed for the time being. This strategy is known as the Closed Sicilian.

One such idea is known as the Grand Prix Attack – 1. e4 c5 2. Nc3 Nc6 3. f4

The Grand Prix Attack, like many closed Sicilian lines, is better understood conceptually rather than by delving into variations, as both sides have plenty of options to continue with. Rather than immediately opening up the center, white accumulates on the kingside.

On the queenside, Black is likely to do the same. Both sides reserve the right to strike in the center later if it becomes advantageous!

3…g6 4. Nf3 Bg7 5. Bc4 e6 6. 0-0 d6 7. d3 Nge7 8. Qe1 a6 9. f5 b5

Hrair - Ivanov, Russia 2006, is a good example of how play can progress! Both sides have an advantage on their respective wings, but white came out on top in this game.

Other options for both sides exist, ranging from the inventive 2. b3 to the subptimal "Bowdler Attack" with 2. Bc4. The Sicilian Defense is a strategically rich and intricate opening. Many White players find the King's Pawn Opening intimidating, but there is a variation for everyone! Learn the Sicilian Defense well, whether you prefer the highly ambitious Open Sicilian, the simple and sound Alapin, the sacrificial Smith-Morra Gambit, or the slow build-up of the Closed Sicilian.
