Endgame Mastery

A Comprehensive Guide to Winning Chess Endgames

By: Fabian Steineck

February 28, 2023

chess strategy games chess tactics

What Is an Endgame in Chess?

The endgame is defined differently depending on who you're talking to. Some theorists believe the endgame begins when both queens are gone, while others believe it begins when one side has less than a certain amount of non-pawn material remaining on the board. The precise definition is less important than the overall characteristics of the endgame:

• Even minor material differences can be decisive.

• The king can now become a powerful attacking piece.

• Players will attempt to promote their pawns into queens.

5 Common Types of Endgames in Chess

1. Basic or fundamental endgames are another name for pawnless endgames. These usually pit one or two pieces against an opposing king. (For example, a king and a queen vs. a king, a king and two bishops vs. a king, and so on.)

2. Rook Endgames (also known as rook-and-pawn endgames) can be highly choreographed affairs, requiring dozens of moves to achieve checkmate. Study the Philidor and Lucena Positions to gain an understanding of these endgames.

3. Endgames featuring two minor pieces against a rook depend heavily on initiative. The exact number of pawns and the pawn structure are critical in these situations.

4. Bishop-and-pawn endings can be exciting and mobile, especially when the bishops share the same color square. However, if the bishops are on opposing colors, you should be prepared to accept a draw.

5. Queen-and-pawn endings are typically lengthy and deliberate. In these games, a queen can easily and quickly escort a passed pawn to the queening square.

Chess Endgame Tips

Position still matters in the endgame, but material differences are more important. More than half of the time, a single pawn difference can represent a decisive advantage (depending on which other material remains in play.) Here are a few general pointers for navigating the endgame.

• In general, chess players who have a material advantage should try to force non-pawn piece exchanges.

• Players with more pawns should try to avoid exchanging pawns. This increases the number of opportunities for advancement.

• In the endgame, initiative is critical. In certain situations, gaining a tempo advantage over an opponent through zugzwang (forcing a player to take a move when they'd rather pass) is worth at least a pawn.

• Aggressive kings and passed pawns tend to provide significant advantages.
